TO: M.   ELIE   GIBERT  CHEZ  M.   GABRIEL   PASTEUR   A   D  ALAIS,
SOURCE: Lines 1484-1566 of LifeLettersAndSpeeches.txt
NOTE: Translated from French

Charleston  4  Septembre  1827.

Sir, Your letter of June 18, 1826, addressed to Mr. Pierre Gibert, has reached me; and I forwarded it to his son, the doctor Joseph Bienaime Gibert, in Longcane. Since it is not certain that you will receive a response from him and I wish to renew relations with the respectable family of my mother, I take the liberty of writing to you. I am unfortunately obliged to begin by informing you of the death of your esteemed brother. He died on June 20, 1815. He was an enlightened, virtuous, and just man. He enjoyed the confidence of his fellow citizens, whom he represented several times at the South Carolina Assembly. His widow died on August 20, 1818. Three sons, Pierre, Clément, and Elie, died young: the first two before their father. Etienne, another son, died in 1823; his widow remained with seven children. Jean Louis, another son, in 1826; his widow remained with six children — both families are more or less prospering.
Those who survived are Lucie, widow Kennedy; Marie, wife of Mr. Wright, a schoolmaster; Harriet, wife of Mr. Hemphill, a Calvinist minister; Elizabeth, wife of Mr. Lee, a landowner; and Joseph Bienaime, a doctor. Mrs. Kennedy and Mrs. Lee are fairly wealthy. Joseph has considerable property, the others are poor. They all live in South Carolina — and most in Longcane.

I need to tell you who I am because my name is probably unknown to you. I am the grandson of Mr. Jean Louis Gibert. He had three children, and I am the son of Louise, the eldest. My dear mother passed away a year ago at the age of 59. We are nine children, four boys and five girls. There are no other descendants of my grandfather. None of the colonists who accompanied him to Longcane survived, but there is a considerable number of their descendants. They have mingled with the other inhabitants and have nearly lost the use of the French language. Three people living in Charleston are the only survivors of those who emigrated with my grandfather. They are Mr. Thomas, Mr. Sabeau, and Mrs. Belot.

I have always had the greatest veneration for the memory of my grandfather, and a very sincere feeling for my mother's relatives. I would be very grateful if you could give me information about the history of the family and the situation in which it is now. Are there many people with the name Gibert? In what part of France do they live? Do they all attend the Reformed Church? Does the family currently provide any ministers for the pulpit? Has Mr. Etienne Gibert of London left any children? And what has become of them?

Without having precisely learned the French language, because my mother, having married an American, spoke only English in the family, I read it quite fluently — I am a lawyer and live in Charleston. I would be very grateful to any of my relatives in France who would honor me by writing to me, and I will always be happy to reply to your letters, if I can persuade you to renew the correspondence that has been interrupted for so long between those of the same lineage who are separated by the sea.

I have the honor to be

Sir  
Your very humble servant  

Sir:

Mr. Elie Gibert having directed that one should write to him at your address, I have taken the liberty of sending you the enclosed; and you will receive the thanks of a stranger, to forward it to him, if he is alive, but if he is no longer, to give it to the eldest of the family.
